Executive Summary

The defining paradox of the U.S. spirits industry in 2025: Americans drank more spirits but spent less doing it. Volume rose 1.9% to 318.1 million cases while supplier revenue fell 2.2% to $36.4 billion — a clear and unmistakable trading-down signal after years of premiumization-driven growth. For the first time in a generation, the spirits industry's growth engine has stalled.

And yet one segment is rewriting the rules. Spirits-based RTDs (ready-to-drink cocktails) surged 16.4% to $3.8 billion, nearly quadrupling in volume over five years from 23.5 million cases to 85.6 million. RTDs are not a sideshow — they are cannibalizing malt-based hard seltzers, stealing occasions from beer, and becoming the default entry point for a generation that drinks less but demands convenience.

On February 20, 2026, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled 6–3 that the president cannot unilaterally impose tariffs under IEEPA, striking down the sweeping duties that had disrupted spirits trade for the past year. For bourbon producers who watched a 50% EU tariff threaten their fastest-growing export market, and for Scotch and Cognac importers absorbing 15–20% surcharges, the ruling provides critical — if fragile — relief.

Meanwhile, the moderation movement is structural, not cyclical. Gen Z drinks 20% less per capita than prior generations. Cannabis beverages are growing at 16.9% CAGR. Nearly half of all Americans planned to drink less in 2025. The spirits industry still commands 42.4% of total beverage alcohol revenue — market share leadership for a fourth consecutive year — but defending that position requires a fundamentally different playbook.

This report examines the forces reshaping the spirits business and identifies the strategies that will define winners and losers in a market that is getting more moderate, more fragmented, and more competitive every year.

Section 2The Tariff Storm — and the Supreme Court Earthquake

Trade policy has been the single most volatile factor in the spirits business for the past two years. The stakes for spirits are enormous: the U.S. exported a record $2.4+ billion in spirits in 2024, with the EU alone accounting for roughly half — including $699 million in American whiskey.

2.1 The Escalation (2025)

  1. Early 2025: Broad U.S. trade tariffs triggered EU retaliation targeting American agricultural and beverage exports.
  2. March 2025: The EU announced a 50% tariff on American whiskey as part of retaliatory duties on $28 billion in U.S. goods.
  3. March 2025: President Trump threatened 200% tariffs on European wine, Champagne, and spirits.
  4. April 2025: Sweeping "reciprocal" tariffs announced under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA), ranging from 10% to 145% depending on the country.
  5. Mid-2025: IEEPA-based tariffs on European spirits imports settled at approximately 15–20%.
  6. Mid-2025: China imposed a 160% retaliatory tariff, effectively shutting off Cognac exports and broader spirits trade.
  7. Mid-2025: Canadian provincial liquor boards removed U.S. spirits from shelves, causing American whiskey exports to Canada to plummet 85%.

The historical precedent is instructive: during Trump's first term (2018–2021), EU/UK retaliatory tariffs caused total U.S. spirits exports to fall 12% and American whiskey exports to fall 18%. After the tariff suspension, producers recaptured most lost market share — but recovery took years.

2.3 The Supreme Court Ruling — February 20, 2026

On February 20, 2026, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled 6–3 in Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump that "IEEPA does not authorize the President to impose tariffs." Chief Justice Roberts authored the majority opinion. The ruling invalidates all IEEPA-based tariffs on imports from the EU, China, Canada, Mexico, and other countries.

What was struck down:

  • All IEEPA-based "reciprocal" tariffs (10–145% on various countries)
  • The 15–20% duties on European spirits imports (Scotch, Cognac, Irish whiskey, etc.)
  • IEEPA tariffs on Mexican imports (affecting tequila/mezcal supply chains)

What remains in place:

  • The EU's 50% retaliatory tariff on American whiskey (separate from IEEPA)
  • China's 160% retaliatory tariff (separate from IEEPA)
  • A new 10% temporary tariff signed the same day under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, effective February 24 for 150 days, with an announced increase to 15%
  • $200+ billion in IEEPA duties already paid may be eligible for refund — but the Court did not rule on this

Trade uncertainty is the new normal.

Vodka ($7.0 Billion): Still the King, but Structurally Vulnerable

Vodka remains the largest spirits category by volume — Americans buy approximately 2.25 times as much vodka as the second-best-selling spirit. But with no clear premiumization narrative and increasing RTD cannibalization, vodka's structural position is eroding. The category needs a reinvention story.

Tequila/Mezcal ($6.4 Billion): The Bull Market Hits a Wall

The defining spirit of the 2020–2023 boom is now in correction. Revenue fell 4.1% while volume slipped only 0.3%, confirming sharp consumer trading-down from super-premium to value 100% agave bottles. A $500+ million agave surplus is reducing raw material costs and enabling lower-priced entries. Celebrity brand saturation is contributing to consumer fatigue. The category is not dying — it is normalizing after years of unsustainable growth.

American Whiskey ($5.1 Billion): The Most Resilient Traditional Category

At just -0.9% revenue decline, American whiskey outperformed every other traditional category. Ultra-premium American whiskey was still growing approximately +6% in 2024. Bourbon's cultural identity, collectability, and export demand give it structural support that other categories lack. The main threat is trade policy: the EU's 50% retaliatory tariff and Canada's shelf removal threaten the $699 million European export business.

Brandy/Cognac: The Tariff Casualty (-10.4%)

The worst-performing traditional category, devastated by China's 160% retaliatory tariff that effectively shut off a critical Cognac export market. Domestic brandy faces additional headwinds from the California wine industry's struggles.

The RTD Revolution ($3.8 Billion, +16.4%)

Spirits-based RTDs are the most important structural shift in the spirits industry:

  • Volume nearly quadrupled in five years: from 23.5M cases (2020) to 85.6M cases (2025)
  • Gained 11 percentage points of market share within spirits in 2025 alone
  • Malt-based hard seltzers lost 14 percentage points — the transfer is direct
  • RTDs now account for roughly 27% of total spirits volume
  • Global RTD market projected to reach $129.8 billion by 2035 (11.5% CAGR)
RTDs are not a fad — they represent a generational shift in how spirits are consumed. They lower the barrier to entry (no bartending skill required), win on convenience (grab-and-go format), and meet consumers at their preferred price point ($2–4 per serving). The question for every major spirits company is no longer whether to invest in RTDs, but how fast.

Spirits RTD Volume Growth (2020–2025)

Millions of 9-liter cases — a nearly 4x increase in five years
The acceleration is steepening, not flattening. RTD volume grew 17.1% in 2025 vs. roughly 15% in 2024. The category is still in its growth phase, not maturation. Industry analyst projections for U.S. spirits-based RTDs suggest a 22.6% CAGR through 2030.

Section 4Consumer Trends: Moderation, Gen Z, and Cannabis

The demand-side story of the spirits industry is being rewritten by three converging forces: generational moderation, the "sober-curious" movement, and cannabis competition.

4.1 Gen Z: The Generation of Moderation

  • Gen Z drinks approximately 20% less alcohol per capita than Millennials or Boomers
  • 21.5% of Gen Z does not consume alcohol at all
  • 39% drink only occasionally
  • 65% planned to drink less in 2025
  • Abstention reasons: 34% cite mental health, 46% cite general lack of interest

The picture is not uniformly bleak. When Gen Z does drink, spirits are their most popular choice, followed by beer and wine. RTDs (42.97%) and hard seltzers (36.25%) are particularly popular entry points. And IWSR reported in December 2025 that Gen Z interest in Dry January is actually fading — suggesting performative abstinence is giving way to organic moderation. Younger legal-drinking-age consumers are "re-engaging with alcohol," just on different terms.

The threat is not that Gen Z will kill the spirits industry. It's that they will buy fewer, better drinks — and increasingly from a can rather than a bottle. The NielsenIQ insight is telling: most on-premise consumers now prefer to buy two premium drinks rather than four standard ones. — NielsenIQ, Five Consumer Trends Shaping U.S. On-Premise Spirit Sales, 2026

4.2 The Broader Moderation Movement

  • Nearly half of all Americans planned to drink less in 2025, up 44% from 2023
  • 81% of Millennials reported drinking in the last 6 months (Autumn 2025) — the lowest ever recorded for that cohort
  • The "less but better" dynamic benefits premium spirits but reduces total volume

4.3 Cannabis: The New Competitor

Hemp-derived THC beverages — broadly legal across states — are entering the same convenience and grocery channels as spirits RTDs, directly competing for the same "moderate social beverage" occasion.

Section 5Opportunities in a Shifting Market

A declining-revenue market is not a dying market. The spirits industry's $36.4 billion in supplier revenue, 42.4% market share lead, and structural advantages over wine and beer create significant opportunities for strategically clear operators.

5.1 The RTD Imperative

Every major spirits company needs an RTD strategy. The math is simple: RTDs grew $537 million in revenue in 2025 while the rest of the spirits category lost roughly $1.3 billion. The brands that own the RTD transition will own the next decade of spirits growth.

  • Diageo's Don Julio is growing 15x faster than the total U.S. spirits industry — partly driven by tequila-based RTD extensions
  • Tito's acquired Lalo Tequila in 2025, its first-ever acquisition, signaling even vodka-dominant brands see the tequila/RTD convergence
  • U.S. spirits-based RTD market projected to grow at 22.6% CAGR through 2030

5.2 The Post-Tariff Export Window

The SCOTUS ruling creates a potential reset for American spirits exports:

  • IEEPA tariffs on imports are struck down, improving economics for Scotch, Cognac, and other import categories
  • If EU retaliatory tariffs are lifted in response, American whiskey regains access to a $699 million export market that grew 59% from 2021–2024
  • Historical precedent: after the first-term tariff suspension, American whiskey producers recaptured most lost market share
  • But the new 10% baseline tariff and unresolved EU/China retaliation mean planning for continued volatility

5.3 Premiumization Is Not Dead — It Is More Selective

Premium and super-premium spirits now represent approximately 35% of total market value, up from 26% a decade ago. The global premium spirits market is projected to reach $388.5 billion by 2034 at 6.4% CAGR. But 2025's tequila data (volume barely down, revenue down 4.1%) shows premiumization has limits when economic pressure mounts.

5.4 DTC: The Regulatory Frontier

Direct-to-consumer spirits shipping remains the industry's largest untapped channel — and its most frustrating regulatory barrier:

  • Only 9 states + D.C. permit DTC spirits shipping (vs. ~47 for wine)
  • 84% of craft spirits drinkers want DTC access; 92% would buy a brand they enjoyed via DTC
  • New York passed DTC spirits delivery legislation in November 2024, opening one of the largest U.S. markets
  • Mobile commerce now drives 75% of online alcohol purchases

5.5 M&A and Consolidation

The spirits industry saw 24 major M&A deals in 2025 out of 51 total beverage alcohol transactions — making spirits the most active M&A category. The dominant theme: large conglomerates divesting non-core assets while acquiring strategic growth brands.

5.6 Craft Distilleries: Scale or Struggle

The U.S. now has over 3,100 distilleries, up from roughly 60 two decades ago. But craft spirits sales posted a second consecutive year of decline in 2025, and craft employment fell for the first time post-pandemic (28,628 vs. 29,373 in 2024). The craft shakeout mirrors what happened in craft beer a decade ago: consolidation, professionalization, and a growing gap between operators who build real brands and those who rely on tasting-room novelty.

The Bottom Line

The U.S. spirits industry is not in crisis — it is in transition. At $36.4 billion and 42.4% market share, spirits remain the revenue leader in American beverage alcohol. But the growth model that worked from 2015 to 2023 — premiumization, premiumization, premiumization — is no longer sufficient.

The winners in 2026 and beyond will be operators who:

  1. Own the RTD transition — spirits-based RTDs are growing 16.4% in a market declining 2.2%. This is not a trend; it is a structural shift in how spirits are consumed.
  2. Build for moderation — lower-ABV products, premium single-serve formats, and quality-over-quantity positioning that meets the "less but better" consumer.
  3. Defend and grow exports — use the post-SCOTUS window to rebuild relationships in the EU and Canada before trade policy shifts again.
  4. Push DTC deregulation — only 9 states allow spirits DTC shipping while 92% of craft drinkers want it. The legislative opportunity is enormous.
  5. Premiumize selectively — ultra-premium American whiskey still works; blanket premiumization across all categories does not. Read the tequila data.
  6. Take cannabis seriously — THC beverages are growing 7x faster than at-home alcohol. Ignore this at your peril.
The spirits industry's first revenue decline in over two decades is not a death sentence — it is a signal. The market is telling you what it wants: convenience, quality, moderation, and formats that fit modern life. The companies that listen will lead the next era. The ones that wait for the old model to come back will be waiting a very long time.
